Module 1: Introduction to MSK Evaluation (20 minutes)

‚úì Complete
## Learning Objectives After completing this module, participants will be able to: - Understand the systematic approach to musculoskeletal evaluation - Recognize the importance of evidence-based examination techniques - Apply clinical reasoning principles to examination planning ## Introduction to Systematic MSK Assessment The musculoskeletal examination forms the foundation of physical therapy practice. A systematic, evidence-based approach ensures comprehensive evaluation while maximizing diagnostic accuracy and clinical efficiency. ### The Examination Framework **1. Subjective History** - Chief complaint and mechanism of injury - Pain characteristics (location, quality, behavior) - Functional limitations and activity restrictions - Previous treatments and interventions - Medical history and red flags **2. Objective Examination** - Observation and postural assessment - Active and passive range of motion - Manual muscle testing - Palpation and tissue assessment - Special tests and neurological screening - Functional movement assessment **3. Clinical Reasoning** - Integration of subjective and objective findings - Formation of clinical hypothesis - Differential diagnosis considerations - Treatment planning and goal setting ### Evidence-Based Special Testing
**Clinical Pearl:** The diagnostic value of special tests is enhanced when combined with thorough history and basic examination findings. No single test should be used in isolation for clinical decision-making.
Understanding test characteristics is essential: - **Sensitivity**: Ability to correctly identify those with the condition - **Specificity**: Ability to correctly identify those without the condition - **Positive Likelihood Ratio (+LR)**: How much a positive test increases probability of diagnosis - **Negative Likelihood Ratio (-LR)**: How much a negative test decreases probability of diagnosis ### Red Flags in MSK Examination
**Immediate Physician Referral Required:** - Cauda equina syndrome signs - Progressive neurological deficits - Signs of fracture or dislocation - Systemic illness indicators - Vascular compromise - Severe night pain with systemic symptoms

Module 2: Cervical Spine (18 minutes)

‚úì Complete
## Learning Objectives After completing this module, participants will be able to: - Perform systematic cervical spine examination - Apply and interpret key cervical spine special tests - Recognize red flags requiring immediate referral ## Subjective Examination Considerations ### Key Historical Elements - Mechanism of injury (flexion, extension, rotation) - Pain distribution and referral patterns - Associated neurological symptoms - Activities that aggravate or relieve symptoms - Previous cervical spine injuries or interventions ### Red Flags for Cervical Spine
**Immediate Medical Attention Required:** - Signs of vertebrobasilar insufficiency - Upper motor neuron signs - Progressive myelopathy - Severe restriction of cervical movement - Drop attacks or transient loss of consciousness
## Objective Examination ### Range of Motion Assessment Normal cervical spine ranges: - Flexion: 50-60¬∞ - Extension: 60-75¬∞ - Side bending: 45¬∞ bilateral - Rotation: 80¬∞ bilateral ### Key Special Tests
**Spurling's Test** - **Purpose**: Cervical radiculopathy - **Technique**: Lateral flexion + compression - **Positive**: Reproduction of arm pain - **Sensitivity**: 30-60% - **Specificity**: 85-100% - **+LR**: 3.5
**Upper Limb Neurodynamic Test (ULNT)** - **Purpose**: Neural mechanosensitivity - **Technique**: Sequential positioning of shoulder, elbow, wrist - **Positive**: Reproduction of symptoms with structural differentiation - **Sensitivity**: 70-83% - **Specificity**: 22-33%
**Deep Neck Flexor Endurance Test** - **Purpose**: Assess deep cervical flexor function - **Technique**: Cranio-cervical flexion with chin tuck - **Normal**: Hold 10 repetitions for 10 seconds - **Clinical significance**: Reduced endurance associated with neck pain
### Clinical Decision Making Cervical radiculopathy diagnosis is enhanced when multiple findings are present: - Positive Spurling's test - Distraction test relieves symptoms - Reduced cervical rotation <60¬∞ - Positive ULNT
**Clinical Pearl:** The combination of age >55, positive Spurling's test, and symptom relief with cervical distraction significantly increases probability of cervical radiculopathy.

Module 3: Thoracic Spine (15 minutes)

‚úì Complete
## Learning Objectives After completing this module, participants will be able to: - Assess thoracic spine mobility and function - Differentiate thoracic spine from cardiac/pulmonary conditions - Apply evidence-based thoracic spine interventions ## Functional Anatomy Considerations The thoracic spine's unique anatomy influences examination approach: - Rib cage attachment limits movement - Kyphotic curvature affects mechanics - Transitional zones (cervicothoracic, thoracolumbar) prone to dysfunction ## Key Examination Components ### Range of Motion - Flexion: 20-45¬∞ - Extension: 15-20¬∞ - Side bending: 20-40¬∞ - Rotation: 35-50¬∞ ### Special Considerations
**Differential Diagnosis Alert:** Thoracic pain may indicate: - Cardiac conditions (angina, MI) - Pulmonary pathology (pneumonia, PE) - Gastrointestinal disorders - Systemic conditions
### Assessment Techniques
**Thoracic Rotation Test** - **Purpose**: Assess thoracic rotation mobility - **Technique**: Seated rotation with arms crossed - **Normal**: 50-60¬∞ bilateral - **Significance**: Limited rotation common in thoracic dysfunction
**Rib Spring Test** - **Purpose**: Assess rib mobility and pain provocation - **Technique**: Posterior-anterior pressure over rib angles - **Positive**: Pain reproduction or restricted mobility - **Clinical use**: Identifying rib dysfunction
### Treatment Considerations Evidence supports: - Thoracic manipulation for neck pain - Thoracic mobility exercises - Postural education and ergonomic modification - Breathing pattern retraining
**Clinical Pearl:** Thoracic spine manipulation can significantly improve cervical spine pain and mobility, even when thoracic symptoms are minimal.

Module 4: Lumbar Spine (20 minutes)

‚úì Complete
## Learning Objectives After completing this module, participants will be able to: - Perform comprehensive lumbar spine examination - Apply clinical prediction rules for lumbar conditions - Recognize serious pathology requiring immediate referral ## Red Flags Assessment
**Cauda Equina Syndrome - Emergency Referral:** - Bilateral sciatica - Saddle anesthesia - Bladder/bowel dysfunction - Progressive motor deficit - Severe or progressive neurological deficit
## Systematic Examination Approach ### Range of Motion Assessment - Flexion: 40-60¬∞ - Extension: 20-35¬∞ - Side bending: 15-20¬∞ bilateral - Rotation: 5-18¬∞ bilateral ### Neurological Screening | Level | Myotome | Dermatome | Reflex | |-------|---------|-----------|--------| | L3 | Quadriceps | Anterior thigh | None | | L4 | Tibialis anterior | Medial leg | Patellar | | L5 | Extensor hallucis longus | Lateral leg/dorsal foot | None | | S1 | Plantar flexors | Lateral foot | Achilles | ### Key Special Tests
**Straight Leg Raise (SLR)** - **Purpose**: Lumbar radiculopathy screening - **Technique**: Passive hip flexion with knee extended - **Positive**: Leg pain <70¬∞ hip flexion - **Sensitivity**: 91% - **Specificity**: 26% - **+LR**: 1.2
**Crossed Straight Leg Raise** - **Purpose**: Disc herniation - **Technique**: SLR on unaffected leg reproduces affected leg pain - **Sensitivity**: 29% - **Specificity**: 88% - **+LR**: 2.4
**Slump Test** - **Purpose**: Neural mechanosensitivity - **Technique**: Progressive spinal flexion, knee extension, ankle dorsiflexion - **Positive**: Symptom reproduction with structural differentiation - **Sensitivity**: 84% - **Specificity**: 83%
### Clinical Prediction Rules **Lumbar Manipulation CPR** (Flynn et al.) Presence of ‚â•4 factors predicts success: 1. Duration of symptoms <16 days 2. No symptoms distal to knee 3. FABQ work subscale score <19 4. Hip internal rotation >35¬∞ (at least one side) 5. Lumbar hypomobility **Lumbar Stabilization CPR** (Hicks et al.) Presence of ‚â•3 factors: 1. Positive prone instability test 2. Aberrant movements present 3. Average SLR >91¬∞ 4. Age <40 years
**Clinical Pearl:** The combination of positive SLR, positive crossed SLR, and appropriate neurological findings significantly increases the likelihood of disc herniation requiring surgical consultation.
## Classification-Based Treatment **Treatment-Based Classification System:** 1. **Manipulation** - Acute, no distal symptoms 2. **Stabilization** - Recurrent episodes, instability signs 3. **Specific Exercise** - Directional preference identified 4. **Traction** - Nerve root compression signs ---

Module 5: Sacroiliac Joint (12 minutes)

‚úì Complete
## Learning Objectives After completing this module, participants will be able to: - Assess sacroiliac joint function and mobility - Apply validated SIJ pain provocation tests - Differentiate SIJ from lumbar spine pathology ## Functional Anatomy The SIJ presents unique examination challenges: - Minimal movement available (2-4mm translation, 1-3¬∞ rotation) - Multiple pain generators in region - Referred pain patterns can mimic lumbar radiculopathy ## Clinical Presentation Typical SIJ pain characteristics: - Unilateral low back/buttock pain - Pain below L5 level - Absence of centralization with repeated movements - Pain with transitional movements (sit-to-stand) ## Examination Strategy ### Pain Provocation Tests
**Posterior Pelvic Pain Provocation Test (P4/Thigh Thrust)** - **Technique**: Hip flexed 90¬∞, posterior force through femur - **Positive**: Reproduction of familiar pain - **Sensitivity**: 88% - **Specificity**: 69% - **+LR**: 2.8
**Distraction Test** - **Technique**: ASIS pressure in supine - **Positive**: Familiar pain reproduction - **Sensitivity**: 60% - **Specificity**: 81% - **+LR**: 3.2
**Compression Test** - **Technique**: Lateral pressure with patient side-lying - **Positive**: Familiar pain reproduction - **Sensitivity**: 69% - **Specificity**: 69% - **+LR**: 2.2
**Sacral Thrust** - **Technique**: Anterior pressure over sacrum (prone) - **Positive**: Familiar pain reproduction - **Sensitivity**: 63% - **Specificity**: 75% - **+LR**: 2.5
### Composite Testing
**Clinical Pearl:** The presence of 3 or more positive SIJ provocation tests significantly increases the likelihood of SIJ-related pain (+LR = 6.8).
**Laslett's SIJ Cluster:** - When ‚â•3 of 5 tests positive: +LR = 6.8 - When <3 tests positive: -LR = 0.16 ### Treatment Considerations Evidence supports: - Manual therapy techniques (manipulation/mobilization) - Specific exercise targeting hip and pelvic stabilizers - Activity modification during acute phases - Patient education regarding aggravating factors ---

Module 6: Shoulder (20 minutes)

‚úì Complete
## Learning Objectives After completing this module, participants will be able to: - Perform systematic shoulder examination including rotator cuff assessment - Differentiate between various shoulder pathologies using special tests - Apply evidence-based examination techniques for shoulder impingement ## Functional Anatomy Review The shoulder complex includes: - Glenohumeral joint - Acromioclavicular joint - Sternoclavicular joint - Scapulothoracic articulation ## Systematic Examination Approach ### Range of Motion Assessment - Flexion: 150-180¬∞ - Extension: 45-60¬∞ - Abduction: 150-180¬∞ - External rotation (arm at side): 45-60¬∞ - Internal rotation: T7-T10 vertebral level ### Rotator Cuff Assessment
**Empty Can Test (Supraspinatus)** - **Technique**: Shoulder abducted 90¬∞, thumbs down, resistance to elevation - **Positive**: Pain or weakness - **Sensitivity**: 64% - **Specificity**: 65% - **+LR**: 1.8
**External Rotation Lag Sign (Infraspinatus)** - **Technique**: Arm positioned in external rotation, release support - **Positive**: Inability to maintain position (lag) - **Sensitivity**: 70% - **Specificity**: 100% - **+LR**: Infinite when positive
**Lift-off Test (Subscapularis)** - **Technique**: Hand behind back, lift off from lumbar spine - **Positive**: Inability to lift hand away from back - **Sensitivity**: 62% - **Specificity**: 100% - **+LR**: Infinite when positive
### Impingement Testing
**Hawkins-Kennedy Test** - **Technique**: Shoulder flexed 90¬∞, forced internal rotation - **Positive**: Pain reproduction - **Sensitivity**: 79% - **Specificity**: 59% - **+LR**: 1.9
**Neer Impingement Sign** - **Technique**: Passive forward elevation with scapula stabilized - **Positive**: Pain in impingement arc - **Sensitivity**: 79% - **Specificity**: 53% - **+LR**: 1.7
### Labral Testing
**O'Brien's Test (Active Compression)** - **Purpose**: SLAP lesion detection - **Technique**: Forward elevation 90¬∞, adduction 15¬∞, thumb down then up - **Positive**: Pain with thumb down, reduced with thumb up - **Sensitivity**: 63% - **Specificity**: 73% - **+LR**: 2.3
### Instability Assessment
**Apprehension Test** - **Technique**: Shoulder abducted 90¬∞, externally rotated - **Positive**: Patient apprehension or guarding - **Sensitivity**: 72% - **Specificity**: 96% - **+LR**: 18
**Clinical Pearl:** Combining multiple rotator cuff tests increases diagnostic accuracy. A positive lag sign is pathognomonic for full-thickness rotator cuff tear.
### Clinical Decision Making **Full-thickness rotator cuff tear probability increases with:** - Age >60 years - Night pain - Weakness in external rotation - Positive lag signs **Shoulder impingement syndrome characteristics:** - Painful arc 60-120¬∞ abduction - Positive Hawkins-Kennedy test - Pain with overhead activities - Response to subacromial injection ---

Module 7: Elbow (12 minutes)

‚úì Complete
## Learning Objectives After completing this module, participants will be able to: - Assess elbow joint function and common pathologies - Differentiate between lateral and medial epicondylalgia - Apply appropriate special tests for elbow examination ## Common Elbow Conditions ### Lateral Epicondylalgia (Tennis Elbow) - Most common elbow overuse injury - Pain over lateral epicondyle with gripping activities - Tenderness over common extensor origin ### Medial Epicondylalgia (Golfer's Elbow) - Pain over medial epicondyle - Associated with repetitive wrist flexion/pronation - Less common than lateral epicondylalgia ## Examination Techniques ### Range of Motion - Flexion: 135-145¬∞ - Extension: 0-5¬∞ hyperextension - Pronation: 75-85¬∞ - Supination: 80-85¬∞ ### Special Tests
**Lateral Epicondylalgia Test** - **Technique**: Resisted wrist extension with elbow extended - **Positive**: Pain over lateral epicondyle - **Clinical utility**: Provocation test for lateral epicondylalgia
**Cozen's Test** - **Technique**: Resisted wrist extension with radial deviation - **Positive**: Pain over lateral epicondyle - **Sensitivity**: 88% - **Specificity**: Not well established
**Mill's Test** - **Technique**: Passive wrist flexion with elbow extension - **Positive**: Pain over lateral epicondyle - **Clinical use**: Stretching test for lateral epicondylalgia
**Medial Epicondylalgia Test** - **Technique**: Resisted wrist flexion with elbow extended - **Positive**: Pain over medial epicondyle - **Clinical utility**: Provocation test for medial epicondylalgia
### Neurological Considerations
**Nerve Entrapment Assessment:** - **Radial tunnel syndrome**: Pain with resisted middle finger extension - **Cubital tunnel syndrome**: Elbow flexion test, Tinel's sign at cubital tunnel - **Pronator syndrome**: Resisted pronation reproduces symptoms
**Clinical Pearl:** Lateral epicondylalgia pain typically worsens with grip strength activities and improves with rest. The condition is often self-limiting but can become chronic without appropriate management.
### Treatment Considerations Evidence-based interventions: - Activity modification and load management - Progressive strengthening exercises - Manual therapy techniques - Patient education regarding aggravating factors ---

Module 8: Wrist and Hand (12 minutes)

‚úì Complete
## Learning Objectives After completing this module, participants will be able to: - Assess wrist and hand function systematically - Apply special tests for common hand/wrist pathologies - Recognize signs requiring specialized hand therapy referral ## Functional Anatomy Considerations The wrist and hand's complex anatomy requires systematic examination: - 8 carpal bones with multiple articulations - Intricate ligamentous support system - Multiple tendon systems (flexors, extensors, intrinsics) - Critical neurovascular structures ## Common Conditions ### Carpal Tunnel Syndrome - Most common nerve entrapment of upper extremity - Median nerve compression at carpal tunnel - Symptoms: numbness/tingling in median nerve distribution ### De Quervain's Tenosynovitis - Inflammation of APL and EPB tendons - Pain over radial styloid process - Associated with repetitive thumb movements ## Examination Techniques ### Range of Motion Assessment **Wrist:** - Flexion: 65-85¬∞ - Extension: 65-85¬∞ - Radial deviation: 15-25¬∞ - Ulnar deviation: 25-35¬∞ ### Carpal Tunnel Assessment
**Phalen's Test** - **Technique**: Sustained wrist flexion for 60 seconds - **Positive**: Numbness/tingling in median nerve distribution - **Sensitivity**: 68% - **Specificity**: 73% - **+LR**: 2.5
**Tinel's Sign at Wrist** - **Technique**: Tapping over carpal tunnel - **Positive**: Tingling in median nerve distribution - **Sensitivity**: 50% - **Specificity**: 77% - **+LR**: 2.2
**Carpal Compression Test** - **Technique**: Direct pressure over carpal tunnel for 30 seconds - **Positive**: Numbness/tingling reproduction - **Sensitivity**: 87% - **Specificity**: 90% - **+LR**: 8.7
### De Quervain's Assessment
**Finkelstein's Test** - **Technique**: Thumb in palm, ulnar deviation of wrist - **Positive**: Pain over radial styloid process - **High sensitivity and specificity for De Quervain's tenosynovitis
### Scaphoid Fracture Screening
**Scaphoid Compression Test** - **Technique**: Axial compression of thumb metacarpal - **Positive**: Pain in anatomical snuffbox - **Clinical significance**: Suggests scaphoid fracture
**Red Flags - Immediate Referral Required:** - Suspected scaphoid fracture - Signs of compartment syndrome - Severe crushing injuries - Complete tendon ruptures - Digital amputations
**Clinical Pearl:** The combination of positive carpal compression test with clinical symptoms provides the strongest evidence for carpal tunnel syndrome diagnosis.
### Treatment Considerations **Carpal Tunnel Syndrome:** - Activity modification and ergonomic training - Night splinting in neutral position - Nerve gliding exercises - Consideration for steroid injection or surgical consultation **De Quervain's Tenosynovitis:** - Activity modification - Thumb spica splinting - Progressive strengthening - Manual therapy techniques ---

Module 9: Hip (15 minutes)

‚úì Complete
## Learning Objectives After completing this module, participants will be able to: - Perform comprehensive hip examination including special tests - Differentiate hip pathology from lumbar spine referred pain - Recognize signs of serious hip pathology requiring imaging ## Functional Anatomy and Biomechanics The hip joint's ball-and-socket configuration provides: - Stability for weight-bearing activities - Mobility for functional movement patterns - Complex muscle interactions for dynamic control ## Common Hip Conditions by Age **Young Adults (16-35):** - Femoroacetabular impingement (FAI) - Labral tears - Athletic injuries **Middle Age (35-55):** - Early osteoarthritis - Greater trochanteric pain syndrome - Hip flexor strains **Older Adults (>55):** - Osteoarthritis - Fractures (especially with osteoporosis) - Referred pain from lumbar spine ## Systematic Examination ### Range of Motion Assessment - Flexion: 110-120¬∞ - Extension: 10-30¬∞ - Abduction: 30-45¬∞ - Adduction: 20-30¬∞ - Internal rotation: 30-40¬∞ - External rotation: 40-60¬∞ ### Key Special Tests
**FABER Test (Patrick's Test)** - **Technique**: Hip flexed, abducted, externally rotated - **Positive**: Groin pain (hip pathology) vs. posterior pain (SIJ) - **Sensitivity**: 57% (hip pathology) - **Specificity**: 71% - **+LR**: 2.0
**FADIR Test (Hip Impingement)** - **Technique**: Hip flexed 90¬∞, adducted and internally rotated - **Positive**: Groin pain reproduction - **Sensitivity**: 78% (labral tears) - **Specificity**: 10% - **+LR**: 0.87
**Trendelenburg Test** - **Technique**: Single-leg stance for 10 seconds - **Positive**: Contralateral pelvis drops - **Indicates**: Hip abductor weakness - **Clinical significance**: Functional impairment indicator
**Thomas Test** - **Technique**: Supine, opposite hip flexed to chest - **Positive**: Test leg rises off table (hip flexor tightness) - **Clinical use**: Assess hip flexor length
### Differential Diagnosis
**Hip vs. Lumbar Spine Differentiation:** - Hip pathology: Groin pain, limited hip internal rotation - Lumbar spine: Back pain with radiation, normal hip ROM - Combined pathology possible, especially in older adults
| Finding | Hip Pathology | Lumbar Spine | |---------|---------------|--------------| | Pain location | Groin/anterior thigh | Back with radiation | | Hip internal rotation | Limited/painful | Usually normal | | SLR test | Negative | May be positive | | FABER test | Groin pain | Posterior pain | ### Serious Pathology Recognition
**Red Flags - Immediate Referral:** - Suspected fracture (trauma, severe pain, inability to bear weight) - Signs of avascular necrosis - Severe night pain with systemic symptoms - Progressive neurological deficits - Infection signs (fever, warmth, severe inflammation)
### Age-Specific Considerations **Femoroacetabular Impingement (FAI):** - Young, active patients - Groin pain with hip flexion activities - Positive FADIR test - May require specialized imaging (MRI arthrogram) **Hip Osteoarthritis:** - Morning stiffness <60 minutes - Crepitus with movement - Limited internal rotation (<25¬∞) - Age >50 years
**Clinical Pearl:** Hip internal rotation limitation is often the first and most sensitive sign of hip joint pathology, particularly osteoarthritis.

Module 10: Knee (18 minutes)

‚úì Complete
## Learning Objectives After completing this module, participants will be able to: - Perform systematic knee examination including ligament and meniscal testing - Apply evidence-based special tests with knowledge of diagnostic accuracy - Recognize acute knee injuries requiring immediate referral ## Functional Anatomy Review The knee's complexity requires systematic examination: - Tibiofemoral joint (primary weight-bearing) - Patellofemoral joint (tracking and stability) - Four major ligaments (ACL, PCL, MCL, LCL) - Two menisci (medial and lateral) - Multiple bursae and fat pads ## History and Mechanism of Injury ### Acute Injuries **Non-contact mechanisms:** - ACL injury: Cutting, pivoting, landing - Meniscal injury: Twisting with planted foot - Patellar dislocation: Valgus stress with external rotation **Contact mechanisms:** - MCL/LCL injury: Direct lateral/medial force - PCL injury: Dashboard injury, direct posterior force - Multiple ligament injury: High-energy trauma ## Systematic Examination ### Range of Motion Assessment - Flexion: 130-140¬∞ - Extension: 0-5¬∞ hyperextension - Note: Any loss of terminal extension is significant ### Ligament Testing
**Anterior Drawer Test (ACL)** - **Technique**: Knee flexed 90¬∞, anterior force on tibia - **Positive**: Increased anterior translation - **Sensitivity**: 55% - **Specificity**: 92% - **+LR**: 6.9
**Lachman Test (ACL)** - **Technique**: Knee flexed 20-30¬∞, anterior force on tibia - **Positive**: Increased anterior translation, soft end-feel - **Sensitivity**: 85% - **Specificity**: 94% - **+LR**: 14.2
**Pivot Shift Test (ACL)** - **Technique**: Complex maneuver with valgus and rotation - **Positive**: Subluxation and reduction felt - **Sensitivity**: 61% - **Specificity**: 97% - **+LR**: 20.3
**Posterior Drawer Test (PCL)** - **Technique**: Knee flexed 90¬∞, posterior force on tibia - **Positive**: Increased posterior translation - **Sensitivity**: 79% - **Specificity**: 100%
**Valgus Stress Test (MCL)** - **Technique**: Knee extended and 30¬∞ flexed, valgus force - **Positive**: Increased medial joint opening - **Grade I**: <5mm opening - **Grade II**: 5-10mm opening - **Grade III**: >10mm opening
**Varus Stress Test (LCL)** - **Technique**: Knee extended and 30¬∞ flexed, varus force - **Positive**: Increased lateral joint opening - **Less common injury than MCL
### Meniscal Testing
**McMurray's Test** - **Technique**: Knee flexed, rotation with extension - **Positive**: Click or pain with manipulation - **Sensitivity**: 16% - **Specificity**: 98% - **+LR**: 8.0 when positive
**Thessaly Test** - **Technique**: Single-leg squat with rotation at 20¬∞ - **Positive**: Medial/lateral joint line pain - **Sensitivity**: 90% - **Specificity**: 97% - **+LR**: 30
### Patellofemoral Assessment
**Patellar Apprehension Test** - **Technique**: Lateral pressure on patella with knee extended - **Positive**: Patient guarding or apprehension - **Indicates**: Patellar instability
**Clarke's Sign** - **Technique**: Patellar compression with quadriceps contraction - **Positive**: Pain prevents contraction - **Note**: High false-positive rate, use cautiously
### Clinical Decision Making **ACL Injury Probability Increases With:** - Positive Lachman test - Positive pivot shift test - Mechanism consistent with ACL injury - Immediate effusion development **Meniscal Injury Considerations:** - Joint line tenderness - Mechanical symptoms (locking, catching) - Positive Thessaly test - MRI most definitive for diagnosis
**Clinical Pearl:** The Lachman test is the most sensitive and specific single test for ACL injury. A positive pivot shift test is highly specific but requires patient relaxation.
### Ottawa Knee Rules **Knee X-ray indicated if ANY of following present:** - Age ‚â•55 years - Isolated tenderness of patella - Tenderness at fibular head - Inability to flex knee to 90¬∞ - Inability to bear weight both immediately and in ED (4 steps)
**Immediate Referral Required:** - Multi-ligament injury (knee dislocation) - Vascular compromise - Compartment syndrome signs - Open fractures - Locked knee unable to extend

Module 11: Ankle and Foot (15 minutes)

‚úì Complete
## Learning Objectives After completing this module, participants will be able to: - Assess ankle and foot injuries using systematic approach - Apply Ottawa Ankle Rules for fracture screening - Differentiate between various ankle pathologies ## Functional Anatomy Considerations The ankle and foot complex includes: - **Talocrural joint**: Primary dorsi/plantarflexion - **Subtalar joint**: Inversion/eversion - **Midfoot**: Multiple small joints for adaptation - **Forefoot**: MTP and IP joints for propulsion ## Common Injury Mechanisms ### Ankle Sprains - **Inversion sprains**: 85% of all ankle sprains - **Eversion sprains**: Less common, potentially more serious - **High ankle sprains**: Syndesmotic injury, longer recovery ### Acute vs. Chronic Conditions **Acute**: Fractures, sprains, Achilles rupture **Chronic**: Tendinopathies, arthritis, posterior impingement ## Systematic Examination ### Range of Motion Assessment - Dorsiflexion: 10-20¬∞ - Plantarflexion: 40-55¬∞ - Inversion: 20-30¬∞ - Eversion: 10-20¬∞ ### Fracture Screening - Ottawa Ankle Rules
**Ottawa Ankle Rules - X-ray Indicated If:** **Ankle X-ray needed if pain in malleolar zone AND:** - Bone tenderness at posterior edge/tip of lateral malleolus OR - Bone tenderness at posterior edge/tip of medial malleolus OR - Inability to bear weight both immediately and in ED **Foot X-ray needed if pain in midfoot zone AND:** - Bone tenderness at navicular OR - Bone tenderness at base of 5th metatarsal OR - Inability to bear weight both immediately and in ED **Sensitivity**: Nearly 100% for fractures **Specificity**: ~40%
### Ligament Testing
**Anterior Drawer Test (ATFL)** - **Technique**: Ankle neutral, anterior force on calcaneus - **Positive**: Increased anterior translation - **Sensitivity**: 71% - **Specificity**: 33% - **Most sensitive test for ATFL injury
**Talar Tilt Test (CFL)** - **Technique**: Ankle neutral, inversion stress - **Positive**: Increased inversion compared to opposite side - **Sensitivity**: 79% - **Specificity**: 74% - **Tests calcaneofibular ligament integrity
**Squeeze Test (Syndesmosis)** - **Technique**: Compression of tibia/fibula at mid-calf - **Positive**: Pain at distal tibiofibular joint - **Sensitivity**: 96% - **Specificity**: 88% - **Indicates high ankle sprain
### Achilles Tendon Assessment
**Thompson Test (Achilles Rupture)** - **Technique**: Prone, squeeze calf muscle - **Positive**: Absence of plantarflexion - **Sensitivity**: 96% - **Specificity**: 93% - **Definitive test for complete Achilles rupture
### Plantar Fascia Assessment
**Windlass Test** - **Technique**: Passive extension of 1st MTP joint - **Positive**: Reproduction of plantar heel pain - **Clinical significance**: Suggests plantar fasciitis
### Tendinopathy Screening **Common locations:** - **Achilles**: Posterior ankle pain, morning stiffness - **Posterior tibial**: Medial ankle pain, arch collapse - **Peroneal**: Lateral ankle pain, especially with eversion
**Clinical Pearl:** Ankle sprains involving syndesmotic injury (high ankle sprain) have significantly longer recovery times and may require different management than typical inversion sprains.
### Clinical Decision Making **Ankle Sprain Grading:** - **Grade I**: Minimal swelling, no instability - **Grade II**: Moderate swelling, mild instability - **Grade III**: Severe swelling, significant instability **Return to Sport Considerations:** - Full pain-free ROM - Normal strength - Functional movement patterns restored - Sport-specific activities tolerated
**Red Flags - Consider Immediate Referral:** - Suspected Achilles rupture - Compartment syndrome signs - Neurovascular compromise - Open fractures - Signs of infection

**Clinical Pearl:** Special tests are most valuable when pre-test probability is moderate (30-70%). Very high or low pre-test probabilities are less influenced by test results.
#### Likelihood Ratios in Practice - **+LR >10**: Large increase in probability - **+LR 5-10**: Moderate increase in probability - **+LR 2-5**: Small increase in probability - **+LR 1-2**: Minimal increase in probability ### Multi-Regional Assessment Many musculoskeletal conditions require examination of multiple regions: **Cervical Radiculopathy**: Cervical spine + upper limb neurodynamics **Lumbar Radiculopathy**: Lumbar spine + lower limb neurodynamics + hip screen **Shoulder Pain**: Shoulder + cervical + thoracic spine **Knee Pain**: Knee + hip + ankle/foot alignment ### Case Integration Examples #### Case 1: 45-year-old with Shoulder Pain **History**: Gradual onset shoulder pain, worse with overhead activities **Key Examination Findings**: - **Emergency Referral Protocols:** - Cauda equina syndrome: Immediate emergency department - Suspected fracture with neurovascular compromise: Immediate orthopedic referral - Signs of serious pathology: Urgent physician consultation - Progressive neurological deficit: Priority referral within 24-48 hours
